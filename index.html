Situation Overview

On February 28, 2026, the US and Israel launched Operation Epic Fury — coordinated airstrikes on Iran targeting military facilities, nuclear sites, and leadership, resulting in the death of Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei. Iran retaliated with missile and drone attacks on Israeli cities and US bases in Gulf states (UAE, Qatar, Bahrain).

The Strait of Hormuz — 21 miles wide at its narrowest, carrying ~20% of global oil and LNG — has become a de facto war zone.

Insurance — The Silent Blockade

Even without a formal closure, the strait is effectively shut because:

  • More than half of the world's largest maritime insurance clubs cancelled war-risk coverage
  • Remaining premiums at multi-year highs
  • Without insurance, ships contractually cannot sail — cargo owners, charterers, and lenders all require it
  • Liberian and Marshall Islands registries (world's #1 and #3) ordered highest security level — cargo ops suspended
"It is de facto closed in that no one dares to go through. You can be attacked, and you can't get insurance or it is extremely expensive." — Arne Lohmann Rasmussen, Global Risk Management

Qatar is world's #2 LNG exporter (~10.8 Bcf/d). This alone = ~20% of global LNG supply offline.
There is no strategic LNG reserve equivalent.
  • Leviathan, Karish, Tamar (Israel) — precautionary shutdown (~3 Bcf/d)

Ports

  • Duqm Port (Oman) — 2 UAV strikes, 1 injured, still operational

Total estimated offline: ~18-20 Bcf/d natural gas (~5% of global supply), 550K+ bpd refining capacity

✅ Already Priced In

  • Maersk/Hapag-Lloyd halting transits
  • Short-term supply disruption (~few days)

❌ NOT Priced In — The Upside

  • Duration beyond 1 week — S&P Global calls this "historic"
  • Duration beyond 25 days — JPMorgan says production shut-ins begin
  • Qatar LNG offline for extended period — generational supply shock for European/Asian gas
  • Insurance companies remaining withdrawn even after hostilities ease — reinsurance takes weeks/months
  • Secondary effects — Houthi restarting Red Sea attacks, Hezbollah opening northern front
  • Strategic reserve drawdowns as price signal (politically constrained)
The Key Variable Is TIME. Markets are pricing a brief shock. Reuters analysis says this optimism "may be misplaced." Every additional day compounds — storage fills, production shuts in, secondary routing maxes out, and insurance stays withdrawn long after shooting stops.

⚠️ Risk Factors / Bear Case

  1. Ceasefire could come fast — Trump could declare victory after degrading Iran's nuclear program, war wraps in days
  2. SPR releases — US (350M+ barrels), China, Japan, Korea can flood market with strategic reserves
  3. OPEC spare capacity — Saudi has ~3M bpd spare (if they can get it out via Yanbu)
  4. Demand destruction — $100+ oil crashes economic activity, self-limiting price ceiling
  5. Many stocks have already moved — you may be buying the top of a fear trade
  6. Iran's navy is destroyed — CENTCOM sank all 11 vessels. Physical blockade limited to mines, shore missiles, drones — disruptive but not sustainable
  7. Insurance could reinstate quickly — if US Navy provides escorts, premiums drop

TL;DR

The strait is de facto closed — not by a physical blockade, but by insurance withdrawal, vessel strikes, and fear. 94% tanker transit collapse in 72 hours. Markets are pricing a brief disruption.

If it lasts >1 week it's "historic" (S&P Global). >25 days it forces production shut-ins (JPMorgan). Trump says operations last "at least four weeks."

Biggest asymmetry: LNG. Qatar offline (~20% of global LNG) has no strategic reserve buffer. LNG (Cheniere) and US gas producers (EQT, AR) benefit most if duration extends. Oil has more buffers (SPR, spare capacity) but tanker stocks benefit from rate spikes regardless.

The single most important variable is duration. Every day of closure compounds exponentially.
